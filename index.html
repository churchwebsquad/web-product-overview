Section 2

What the Web Offering Is

We build your church a website that sounds like you, looks like you, and serves the people you're trying to reach — both the ones walking through your doors and the ones finding you online for the first time.

Every project starts with your online brand guide from the Brand Squad — whether that's a brand new identity or your existing brand formatted into a consistent guide. We study it closely and make sure the site feels like a real extension of who you are. From there, we handle strategy, copywriting, design, and the full WordPress build.

Your site launches with your core pages — the ones your visitors need most. If you're working toward a specific date, we prioritize those pages and get you live, then continue building out the rest after launch. If there's no rush, we build everything together and launch the complete site at once. Either way, we build with future updates in mind — your content is structured so it's easy to keep current.

After launch, the site is yours. You get admin access and full ownership. We provide hosting and ongoing web support through your All-In plan — so when something needs updating or you want to make a change, we're right there.

What's Included

✅ Always included in a Redesign

  • Complete WordPress rebuild
  • Full strategy: UX, copy, SEO optimization

Web Pathway Updates

We've simplified and streamlined the web offering into one clear pathway: Web. Previously, the pathway guide listed four options: Redesign, Microsite, Refresh, and Strategic Audit. Now it flows through a single track: an optional audit to understand your options → an optional redesign (if you choose to keep your current site, skip to support) → ongoing web support. Here's what changed and what it means in practice.

An optional first step — not the start of the pathway itself. It's a quick overview of how a partner's site is performing today, designed to help them understand their options and point them to the right services within CMS — whether that's a redesign, a support request, or both. Partners can skip it entirely and go straight to what they need.

Phase 1 Launch (within Redesign)
Phase 1 prioritizes 8 core pages, ready to launch as an MVP version of the site to prioritize speed. Phase 2 builds out remaining pages post-launch — or continues in the background if the partner opts to hold launch. This phased workflow is still being formalized; today we can launch a core page site, but the workflow doesn't have a dedicated task set for it yet. We're currently still using the microsite terminology at discovery.
A note on language We're transitioning away from the term "microsite" in partner-facing conversations — it's insider language that can make the work sound smaller than it actually is. Instead, use phrases like "We'll launch your core pages first and build out from there," "a Phase 1 launch," or "a foundation launch." The work is the same — the language just better reflects the value of what we're delivering.

Redesign Fit — Quick Checklist for AMs

A redesign is the right path when the church:

✅ Points toward Redesign

  • Wants to switch to WordPress
  • Has structural changes in mind
  • Wants new content strategy or full copy
  • Needs mobile or SEO improvements
  • Has flexibility to work through our full redesign process
  • Wants a visual upgrade beyond colors/logos

➡ Points toward Support Request instead

  • Just wants logos, colors, or fonts updated
  • Wants to add or update a page or section
  • Site is solid — they want targeted tweaks
  • No platform or structural change needed

Consolidated Reviews Updated

We've condensed the partner review process from 4 checkpoints to 3 by combining the copy review and design review into a single, combined Review 2. Here's why this matters and how it works:

Design + Copy Review

This review was already part of the workflow, but previously included fully designed pages. Now, Review 2 includes the full written copy alongside only the 6 Phase 1 pages designed — the pages we'd launch with if the partner wants to proceed at that step. The partner reviews both copy and design together. Once approved, copy is locked. Any text changes at Review 3 should be minor tweaks (QA-level), not structural rewrites.

Pre-framing the partner's feedback at Review 2 We give the partner specific prompts to guide their review:
"Please review the visual design in MarkUp. Does this feel like your church?"
"Please read the full copy in MarkUp. Do the words sound like your church's voice, brand, and tone?"
This ensures we get real copy feedback before the rest of design and development begins.

Timeline Protection

Church leaders are naturally inclined to request custom tweaks because they see their ministry as unique. If a partner asks to see "just one more page" during the Design + Copy Review, or requests a major structural change at Review 3, AMs have a script to keep the project on track while remaining helpful:

"We absolutely want to get this right for you. We can make that structural change, but it will move your project outside of our standard framework and into a custom build timeline. Would you prefer to launch on schedule, or adjust the timeline to accommodate this change?"

Final Review Ownership

The Content Strategist serves as the central triage point for Review 3 feedback. All partner notes get routed into one place, then audited and assigned to the right person: copy edits go to the Content Strategist, visual tweaks go to the Web Designer, and layout or functional items go to the WordPress Developer. If a text change would break a design layout, copy gets adjusted first, then design finalizes spacing. No one touches the staging site until assigned their specific piece.

Section 6

Setting Realistic Timelines

Timeline conversations are one of the most common places where partner expectations get miscalibrated. Here's where we are and where we're headed.

Where We're Headed

6–8 week workflow
This is the goal we're actively building toward. With the condensed 3-review milestone structure, the template library, and the tools being developed in Section 5 above, we're working to get redesign timelines down to 6–8 weeks. We're not there yet — the systems and tools need to be fully built out first — but this is what we're aiming for.

Where We Are Today

Timeline varies
Right now, timeline depends on project complexity and how quickly we move through feedback cycles together with the partner. The condensed review structure helps, and Phase 1 launches let us get core pages live sooner while the full site builds out.

How We Get There

Faster to first live site
Phase 1 prioritizes 8 core pages, ready to launch as an MVP version of the site to prioritize speed. Phase 2 builds out remaining pages post-launch — or continues in the background if the partner opts to hold launch.

What Needs to Happen

The 6–8 week target depends on the template library, component asset system, and automation tools described in Section 5 being fully operational. As those come online, timelines will continue to improve.
