Before We Dive In

Why This Document Exists

The web offering is evolving — and that's a great thing. Web 2.0 represents a real step forward in how we serve our partners: a faster, more focused, strategy-first approach to building church websites. As the offering has grown, we want to make sure everyone on the team feels confident and equipped to talk about it.

This document is our single source of truth. It's here so that when a partner or prospective church asks about our website services, we're all speaking the same language. It's also a living document — if you spot something that doesn't match what's here, we want to hear about it. More on that at the end.

The short version Our web offering is centered around one clear pathway: Website Redesign. Visual updates are handled through web support requests. A strategic audit is available as an optional first step to help partners identify what they need. And for churches that need to launch quickly, we can launch core pages first and expand from there. Everything below explains how it all works together.
Section 1

What the Web Offering Is

The All-In web offering is a strategy-first website partnership for churches. We don't just build websites — we help churches understand what their site needs to accomplish, then we build, design, write, and launch it for them. Partners stay with us after launch for ongoing support through their All-In subscription.

Strategy

We think through structure, content, and user experience before a single pixel is placed.

Design + Copy

Custom design in Figma, branded to the church. Original copywriting written by our content strategist.

Build + Launch

WordPress + Bricks, hosted on Pressable. We handle the technical build and launch process end-to-end.

What's Included

✅ Always included in a Redesign

  • Complete WordPress rebuild
  • Full strategy: UX, copy, SEO optimization
  • Custom design in Figma
  • Bricks builder with Church Plugins
  • Hosting on our managed Pressable platform
  • Ongoing web support after launch (All-In)

❌ Not included

  • E-commerce or app builds
  • Platform support outside WordPress
  • Custom integrations outside Church Plugins
  • Development on Squarespace, Subsplash, etc.
  • App design

A note on language We're transitioning away from the term "microsite" in partner-facing conversations — it's insider language that can make the work sound smaller than it actually is. Instead, use phrases like "We'll launch your core pages first and build out from there," "a Phase 1 launch," or "a foundation launch." The work is the same — the language just better reflects the value of what we're delivering.

Redesign Fit — Quick Checklist for AMs

A redesign is the right path when the church:

✅ Points toward Redesign

  • Wants to switch to WordPress
  • Has structural changes in mind
  • Wants new content strategy or full copy
  • Needs mobile or SEO improvements
  • Has flexibility to work through our full redesign process
  • Wants a visual upgrade beyond colors/logos

➡ Points toward Support Request instead

  • Just wants logos, colors, or fonts updated
  • Wants to add or update a page or section
  • Site is solid — they want targeted tweaks
  • No platform or structural change needed

What Changed: 3 Reviews Instead of 4 Updated

We've condensed the partner review process from 4 checkpoints to 3 by combining the copy review and design review into a single, combined Review 2. Here's why this matters and how it works:

The "Appetizer" Approach

At Review 2, the partner receives the entire copy and the homepage + 5 key pages designed — presented together. It's one review, but they're looking at two things: the full written copy and the visual design applied to key pages. This helps partners see how copy and design build on each other, and it creates a much more exciting early reveal.

Why This Works

When partners see a beautiful, high-fidelity design for the first time, they can get distracted by the visuals and forget to read the words. By sending both together with clear review prompts, we guide their attention. Once Review 2 is approved, copy is locked. Any text changes at Review 3 should be minor tweaks (QA-level), not structural rewrites.

Pre-framing the partner's feedback at Review 2 We give the partner specific prompts to guide their review:
"Please review the visual design in MarkUp. Does this feel like your church?"
"Please read the full copy in MarkUp. Do the words sound like your church's voice, brand, and tone?"
This ensures we get real copy feedback before the rest of design and development begins.

Scope Shift: Protecting the Timeline

Church leaders are naturally inclined to request custom tweaks because they see their ministry as unique. If a partner asks to see "just one more page" during the Appetizer review, or requests a major structural change at Review 3, AMs have a script to keep the project on track while remaining helpful:

"We absolutely want to get this right for you. We can make that structural change, but it will move your project outside of our standard framework and into a custom build timeline. Would you prefer to launch on schedule, or adjust the timeline to accommodate this change?"

Review 3: Triage Process

The Content Strategist serves as the central triage point for Review 3 feedback. All partner notes get routed into one place, then audited and assigned to the right person: copy edits go to the Content Strategist, visual tweaks go to the Web Designer, and layout or functional items go to the WordPress Developer. If a text change would break a design layout, copy gets adjusted first, then design finalizes spacing. No one touches the staging site until assigned their specific piece.

For AMs setting expectations When partners ask about reviews, let them know: "You'll have three moments to review our work. First, you'll review the sitemap and strategy. Then, you'll see your full copy alongside your homepage and key pages designed — so you can see how everything comes together. Finally, you'll review the full site before we launch."

Section 6

Setting Realistic Timelines

Timeline conversations are one of the most common places where partner expectations get miscalibrated. Here's where we are and where we're headed.

Where We're Headed

6–8 week workflow
This is the goal we're actively building toward. With the condensed 3-review milestone structure, the template library, and the tools being developed in Section 5 above, we're working to get redesign timelines down to 6–8 weeks. We're not there yet — the systems and tools need to be fully built out first — but this is what we're aiming for.

Where We Are Today

Timeline varies
Right now, timeline depends on project complexity and how quickly we move through feedback cycles together with the partner. The condensed review structure helps, and Phase 1 launches let us get core pages live sooner while the full site builds out.

Phase 1 Launch

Faster to first live site
Core pages (~8) go live first. Full site is already designed — Phase 2 pages build out after launch.

What Needs to Happen

The 6–8 week target depends on the template library, component asset system, and automation tools described in Section 5 being fully operational. As those come online, timelines will continue to improve.

Common Add-ons

+2 weeks: Pages 16–20
+2 weeks: Pages 21–25
+2 weeks: Custom post templates
+2–4 weeks: Advanced features
+1–2 weeks/yr: Content migration

"Our redesign process is designed to move efficiently — and we're continuing to improve our tools to make it even faster. The timeline will depend on your site's complexity and how quickly we're able to move through feedback cycles together. The good news is we can often get your core pages live quickly while we continue building out the rest."
